But rings can be very complex objects and may interact in tricky ways with
stellar and planetary light. They are granular in nature (and hence
semitransparent) favouring the diffuse reflection of incoming stellar light and
potentially polarizing it. They are not continuous and may have large
divisions and/or ringlets. Depending on ring inclination and orientation, rings
may cast large shadows on planetary surface and vice versa: a significant
fraction of the ring can be permanently shadow by the planet. Disregarding all
these effects will have an impact on any calculated light-curve. Taking most
of them into account would render reflected\footnot{Occasionally e will refer
to backward scattering as ``reflection'' without necessarily implying that we
assume that light is coherently reflected on any surface.} light-curves in an
incredible source of information.
This is precisely what this paper aims: to present and test a model of the
reflected light-curve produced by a ringed planet with arbitrary physical
properties and geometrical configuration. \rev{In Section \ref{sec:model} we
introduce the basic idea behind the model, namely the discretization of the
objects involved in {\it spangles}, circular plain disks covering their
surfaces. One of the key characteristics of the model is that the light
scattering is computed in an object-centered reference frame. In Section
(\ref{sec:rf}) we describe the geometrical details of the model. We show in
Section \ref{sec:sintetic_lc} some synthetic light curves and illustrate their
main features. Then, we fit in Section \ref{sec:fit_test} the present model to
the light curve of Kepler-1704 b in the search for planetary rings. Finally, we
discuss in Section \ref{sec:discussion} the main applications of our model.}
\section{The spangles model}
\label{sec:model}
%We present in this paper a planet-centric formalism for a model that includes
second-order effects such as the shadow/shine of the planet's surface and
atmosphere; forward scattering, the rings' opacity, reflection of light by
dusty/icy ring-particles. All of these effects can be computed for any
orientation of planetary systems, and the results of this work will allow us to
constrain and obtain {\em fiducial} orbital/physical parameters of real
planetary systems where fitting the phase curves of ringed planets stands out
as a very feasible option.
